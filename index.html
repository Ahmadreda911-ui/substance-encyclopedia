01 — Definition & Classification

What is a Psychoactive Substance?

From opium in ancient Sumeria to synthetic designer drugs engineered in clandestine laboratories, substances that alter human consciousness represent one of humanity's oldest and most complex relationships.

The Scientific Definition

A psychoactive drug or psychoactive substance is a chemical substance that changes brain function and results in alterations in perception, mood, consciousness, cognition, or behavior. These substances act on the central nervous system (CNS) by interacting with specific receptor proteins, ion channels, enzymes, or transport proteins.

The World Health Organization (WHO) defines a drug more broadly as "any substance which, when taken into a living organism, may modify one or more of its functions." This encompasses not only illicit substances but also prescription medications, alcohol, nicotine, and caffeine.

"The distinction between a medicine and a poison is often a matter of dose, context, and the society in which it is consumed."

Psychoactive substances achieve their effects through a variety of pharmacological mechanisms — acting as agonists, antagonists, reuptake inhibitors, enzyme inhibitors, or allosteric modulators of the brain's endogenous chemical signaling systems. Understanding these mechanisms is fundamental to both neuropharmacology and clinical medicine.

⚗️

Pharmacodynamics refers to how a drug affects the body — its mechanism of action at target receptors. Pharmacokinetics refers to how the body processes the drug — absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME). Together, these two fields define the complete pharmacological profile of any substance.

02 — Historical Perspective

A 10,000-Year Relationship

The human relationship with psychoactive substances predates written history. Archaeological evidence suggests our ancestors used mind-altering plants in ritual, medicinal, and social contexts long before civilization as we know it.

~8000 BCE
Neolithic Period
The First Fermented Beverages
Archaeological evidence from the Zagros Mountains (modern Iran) reveals the earliest known production of fermented grape wine. Residue analysis of pottery shards indicates deliberate alcoholic fermentation, suggesting our Neolithic ancestors understood fermentation technology some 10,000 years ago. Simultaneously, opium poppy cultivation began in the Western Mediterranean region.
🍷 Alcohol & Opium Origins
3400 BCE
Early Mesopotamia
Sumerian Opium Records — "Hul Gil"
Sumerian texts reference the opium poppy (Papaver somniferum) as "hul gil" — the "joy plant." Cultivation was widespread across Lower Mesopotamia. This represents the first documented human use of an opioid substance, demonstrating early understanding of the poppy's pain-relieving and euphoria-inducing properties. The Sumerians likely understood tolerance and withdrawal, as evidenced by controlled cultivation patterns.
📜 First Written Drug Record
2700 BCE
Ancient China
Emperor Shen Nung's Pharmacopoeia
The legendary Chinese Emperor Shen Nung catalogs over 300 plants in his pharmacopoeia, including cannabis (ma), which he recommends for over 100 ailments including rheumatism, gout, and malaria. He is also credited with discovering the stimulant properties of ephedra (ma huang) and tea. His texts represent the first systematic attempt to classify psychoactive substances by medical application.
🌿 Cannabis & Ephedra
1200 BCE
Ancient Egypt & Greece
The Ebers Papyrus and Eleusinian Mysteries
The Ebers Papyrus, one of the oldest medical documents, contains hundreds of prescriptions including opium preparations for pain relief and sedation. In Greece, the Eleusinian Mysteries — sacred religious rites practiced for nearly 2,000 years — are now believed by scholars to have involved a psychedelic barley brew (kykeon) containing ergot alkaloids precursors to LSD, inducing the mystical visions central to the ceremony.
🏛️ Ritual & Medicine
1500s CE
Age of Exploration
Columbian Exchange of Psychoactives
European colonization precipitated the global spread of psychoactive substances. Tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) was introduced to Europe from the Americas, spreading worldwide within decades. Simultaneously, coffee from Ethiopia reached Arabia and then Europe, transforming social life. Coca leaves, used for millennia by Andean civilizations for altitude sickness and fatigue, were observed and later exported — laying the groundwork for cocaine's eventual isolation.
🌍 Globalization of Drugs
1804
Early Modern Chemistry
Isolation of Morphine — The Birth of Alkaloid Chemistry
Friedrich Sertürner, a German pharmacist's apprentice, isolated morphine from opium — the first time an active principle was extracted from a plant. Named after Morpheus, the Greek god of dreams, morphine was 10 times more potent than opium and could be precisely dosed. This breakthrough launched the era of alkaloid chemistry, leading to the isolation of quinine (1820), caffeine (1820), nicotine (1828), codeine (1832), and cocaine (1859). The pharmaceutical industry as we know it was born.
⚗️ Alkaloid Chemistry Revolution
1856–1906
Victorian Era
The Age of Patent Medicines and Opium Wars
The Victorian era witnessed rampant unregulated drug use. Patent medicines containing morphine, cocaine, heroin, and laudanum were sold over-the-counter to adults and children alike. Coca-Cola originally contained cocaine (removed in 1903). Bayer marketed heroin in 1898 as a "non-addictive" morphine substitute and cough suppressant. Simultaneously, the British Empire fought two Opium Wars (1839–1842, 1856–1860) against China to maintain its profitable opium trade, demonstrating the geopolitical dimensions of drug commerce.
💊 Unregulated Era & Geopolitics
1912–1971
International Control Begins
From Harrison Act to the UN Single Convention
The International Opium Convention (1912) marked the beginning of global drug control. The US Harrison Narcotics Tax Act (1914) effectively criminalized non-medical use of opioids and cocaine. Albert Hofmann accidentally discovered LSD's psychedelic properties in 1943 at Sandoz Laboratories. The UN Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961) and the Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971) established the modern international drug control framework, classifying substances into schedules and obligating signatory nations to criminalize non-medical use.
⚖️ Prohibition Era
1971
Nixon Administration
The "War on Drugs" Declared
US President Richard Nixon declared drug abuse "public enemy number one" and launched the War on Drugs, allocating significant resources to supply reduction and criminalization. This era saw the establishment of the DEA (1973), mandatory minimum sentencing, and the escalation of drug-related mass incarceration. Decades later, Nixon's domestic policy chief John Ehrlichman acknowledged that the War on Drugs was politically motivated to target anti-war hippies and Black communities, representing one of the most consequential admissions in US political history.
🏛️ War on Drugs
1980–2000
Crack Epidemic & Rave Culture
Crack Cocaine and the Rise of Designer Drugs
The crack cocaine epidemic devastated urban communities across America in the 1980s, exacerbating racial disparities in drug law enforcement (powder vs. crack cocaine sentencing disparities of 100:1). Simultaneously, MDMA ("ecstasy"), synthesized by Merck in 1912 but rediscovered by chemist Alexander Shulgin in 1976, became the defining drug of rave culture. Shulgin's books PIHKAL and TIHKAL systematically documented hundreds of novel psychedelic compounds, laying groundwork for the "designer drug" era.
💊 Crack, MDMA & Designer Drugs
1999–2020s
Opioid Epidemic
The Opioid Crisis — A Manufactured Epidemic
Purdue Pharma's aggressive marketing of OxyContin beginning in 1996 — falsely claiming it had low addiction potential — ignited the American opioid epidemic. Over 500,000 Americans died from opioid overdoses between 1999 and 2019. As prescription opioids were restricted, users shifted to heroin and then to illicitly manufactured fentanyl — 50-100x more potent than morphine — leading to unprecedented overdose rates. Purdue Pharma pleaded guilty to federal criminal charges in 2020, paying $8.3 billion in penalties.
⚠️ Modern Opioid Crisis
2010s–Present
Psychedelic Renaissance
The Return of Therapeutic Research
After decades of suppression following Schedule I classification, rigorous clinical research on psychedelic-assisted therapy has experienced a renaissance. Johns Hopkins, NYU, and Imperial College London published landmark studies demonstrating psilocybin's efficacy for treatment-resistant depression, tobacco addiction, and end-of-life anxiety. MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD completed Phase 3 trials with remarkable results. In 2024, Australia became the first country to permit prescribed psychedelic therapy. Oregon legalized supervised psilocybin use in 2020. The scientific paradigm around these substances is fundamentally shifting.
🔬 Psychedelic Renaissance
03 — Drug Categories

Major Classes of Psychoactive Substances

Each class of drug produces distinct neurochemical changes, leading to characteristic subjective effects, therapeutic potential, and risk profiles. Understanding these differences is essential to rational drug policy and clinical practice.

Class I — Opioids

Opioids & Opiates

Opioids are a class of compounds that bind to opioid receptors — principally μ (mu), κ (kappa), and δ (delta) — distributed throughout the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral tissues. They are the most powerful analgesics known to medicine and also carry the highest risk of physical dependence and fatal overdose.

Natural opiates (morphine, codeine) are derived directly from the latex of Papaver somniferum. Semi-synthetic opioids (heroin/diacetylmorphine, oxycodone, hydrocodone) are chemically modified natural opiates. Fully synthetic opioids (fentanyl, methadone, tramadol) are entirely human-made with no natural analog. Fentanyl analogs like carfentanil — designed for elephant tranquilization — are an estimated 10,000× more potent than morphine.

The opioid epidemic has killed over 500,000 Americans since 1999, making it one of the deadliest drug crises in recorded history. The primary risk of opioid overdose is respiratory depression — the brainstem's drive to breathe is suppressed, leading to hypoxia, coma, and death. The antidote naloxone (Narcan) is a competitive opioid receptor antagonist that rapidly reverses this effect.

Class II — Psychostimulants

Stimulants

Stimulants increase activity in the central nervous system by enhancing monoamine neurotransmitter activity — specifically dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin. Their primary mechanism involves blocking reuptake transporters (DAT, NET, SERT), forcing neurotransmitters to remain in the synapse longer, or triggering reverse transport, flooding the synapse with monoamines.

Cocaine acts as a nonselective monoamine reuptake inhibitor with rapid onset via insufflation or inhalation (crack). Amphetamine and methamphetamine both block reuptake and trigger reverse transport, producing more sustained and intense dopamine release. MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine) has a unique profile — it primarily acts on SERT, flooding synapses with serotonin, producing the characteristic empathogenic effects distinct from classic stimulants.

Medically, stimulants like methylphenidate (Ritalin) and amphetamine salts (Adderall) are first-line treatments for ADHD and narcolepsy. Their paradoxical calming effect in ADHD patients is attributed to normalization of dysfunctional prefrontal cortical dopamine signaling.

Class III — CNS Depressants

Depressants

CNS depressants slow brain activity by enhancing the inhibitory effects of GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid), the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. The GABA-A receptor is a chloride ion channel — when GABA binds, the channel opens, allowing Cl⁻ influx that hyperpolarizes the neuron, reducing its likelihood of firing.

Alcohol (ethanol) is a positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptors and simultaneously inhibits excitatory NMDA glutamate receptors. Benzodiazepines (diazepam/Valium, alprazolam/Xanax) bind to the GABA-A receptor at the benzodiazepine binding site, increasing the frequency of chloride channel opening. Barbiturates bind to a different GABA-A site and increase the duration of channel opening — they are more dangerous because at high doses they can activate GABA-A receptors without GABA present.

Depressant withdrawal is particularly dangerous — abrupt cessation after physical dependence can cause life-threatening seizures and delirium tremens (DTs), because the brain's excitatory systems have been chronically upregulated to compensate for the drug's inhibitory effects.

Class IV — Classical Hallucinogens

Psychedelics & Hallucinogens

Classical psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, mescaline, DMT) are serotonergic hallucinogens that act as full or partial agonists at the 5-HT₂A serotonin receptor in the prefrontal cortex. This receptor modulates glutamate release, synaptic plasticity, and cortico-cortical communication. The subjective effects — visual and auditory hallucinations, profound alterations in consciousness, ego dissolution — arise from massively increased neural signal diversity and disrupted default mode network (DMN) suppression.

LSD (lysergic acid diethylamide), synthesized by Albert Hofmann in 1938, is active at doses as low as 25 micrograms — making it among the most potent psychoactive substances by mass. Psilocybin, the prodrug found in "magic mushrooms," is dephosphorylated to psilocin in vivo; psilocin is structurally similar to serotonin. DMT (dimethyltryptamine) is endogenously produced in mammals and is the active component of ayahuasca.

Critically, classical psychedelics have no known lethal dose in humans from pharmacological toxicity, and they are non-addictive — they do not trigger dopaminergic reward circuits. Current research demonstrates striking therapeutic potential for depression, addiction, and PTSD.

Class V — Cannabinoids

Cannabis & Cannabinoids

Cannabinoids act on the endocannabinoid system (ECS) — a neuromodulatory system present throughout the brain and body, consisting of CB1 and CB2 receptors, endogenous ligands (anandamide and 2-AG), and enzymes for their synthesis and degradation. The ECS plays a fundamental role in regulating appetite, pain, mood, memory, and immune function.

THC (Δ9-tetrahydrocannabinol) is the primary psychoactive component, acting as a partial agonist at CB1 receptors — predominantly located in the brain, particularly in the hippocampus, basal ganglia, cerebellum, and prefrontal cortex. CBD (cannabidiol) is largely non-psychoactive, acting as a negative allosteric modulator of CB1 and having anxiolytic, anti-inflammatory, and anticonvulsant properties. The "entourage effect" hypothesis suggests that the 100+ cannabinoids and 200+ terpenes in cannabis work synergistically.

Cannabis is the most widely used illicit drug globally with approximately 209 million annual users. It is now legal for recreational use in 24 US states, Canada, Germany, and several other countries, reflecting a seismic shift in drug policy. Medical applications include chronic pain, chemotherapy-induced nausea, multiple sclerosis spasticity, and treatment-resistant epilepsy (Epidiolex).

Class VI — Dissociatives

Dissociatives

Dissociatives produce a unique state of consciousness characterized by detachment from reality, depersonalization, derealization, and at high doses, complete dissociative anesthesia. The primary mechanism is NMDA receptor antagonism — blocking the NMDA glutamate receptor, the primary mediator of excitatory synaptic transmission and synaptic plasticity.

Ketamine was approved as a surgical anesthetic in 1970 and remains on the WHO List of Essential Medicines. At sub-anesthetic doses, it produces dissociative and hallucinogenic effects. In 2019, the FDA approved esketamine (Spravato) — the S-enantiomer of ketamine — as the first genuinely novel antidepressant mechanism in 60 years, acting through NMDA antagonism to rapidly (within hours) reverse treatment-resistant depression. PCP (phencyclidine) is more potent and dangerous, with a complex pharmacology that also includes sigma receptor agonism and dopamine reuptake inhibition, producing psychotic states that mimic schizophrenia. DXM (dextromethorphan) is a dissociative found in over-the-counter cough medicines that is widely abused, particularly by adolescents.

04 — Neurobiological Mechanisms

How Drugs Hijack the Brain

At their core, all psychoactive drugs work by mimicking, blocking, or modulating the brain's own signaling molecules. Understanding these mechanisms explains both the profound subjective effects of drugs and the neurological basis of addiction.

REWARD PATHWAY — MESOLIMBIC CIRCUIT
⚡
The Dopamine Reward Circuit
Mesolimbic pathway — addiction's core

The mesolimbic dopamine pathway — from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens (NAc) — is the brain's primary reward circuit. Natural rewards (food, sex, social bonding) release modest amounts of dopamine in the NAc. Drugs of abuse release 2–10× more dopamine than natural rewards, creating an overwhelming signal that says "do this again." Repeated drug use dysregulates this system, reducing baseline dopamine signaling and creating a state of anhedonia that drives compulsive drug-seeking to restore normal dopamine levels.

🔄
Neuroplasticity & ΔFosB
Long-term molecular changes

Chronic drug exposure induces long-lasting neuroplastic changes. The transcription factor ΔFosB accumulates in the nucleus accumbens with repeated drug exposure and persists for weeks to months after cessation. It acts as a "molecular switch" that alters gene expression, reducing sensitivity to natural rewards while enhancing sensitivity to drug cues. BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) expression is also altered, affecting synaptic density and architecture in the prefrontal cortex and limbic system.

🧩
Prefrontal Control & Craving
The loss of executive function

Addiction involves a fundamental impairment in prefrontal cortical (PFC) regulation of limbic impulses. In healthy individuals, the PFC exerts top-down inhibitory control over the amygdala and NAc, enabling rational decision-making. Chronic drug use reduces gray matter volume and functional connectivity in the PFC — specifically the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) — impairing impulse control, decision-making, and the ability to resist drug cues even when consequences are understood.

📊
Tolerance & Allostatic Adaptation
The biology of "needing more"

Tolerance develops through receptor downregulation (fewer receptors expressed), desensitization (receptors become less responsive), and compensatory neuroadaptation (opposing systems upregulate). George Koob's allostatic theory of addiction describes addiction as a chronic shift in the brain's hedonic setpoint — users feel progressively worse when not using (negative emotional states, anxiety, dysphoria) because the brain has recalibrated around the drug's presence. This withdrawal-driven negative reinforcement is often more powerful than the positive reinforcement of the "high."

Key Neurotransmitter Systems

Molecular Targets of Psychoactive Drugs

⚡
Dopamine
C₈H₁₁NO₂
The primary reward and motivation neurotransmitter. Targeted by stimulants (cocaine, amphetamine), opioids, nicotine, and alcohol. The mesolimbic dopamine pathway mediates the reinforcing effects of virtually all drugs of abuse.
🔵
Serotonin
C₁₀H₁₂N₂O
Regulates mood, cognition, sleep, and appetite. Primary target of SSRIs, MDMA, and classical psychedelics (via 5-HT2A). Depleted after MDMA use ("comedowns"). Implicated in depression, anxiety disorders.
🟡
GABA
C₄H₉NO₂
The primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. Enhanced by alcohol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates, and GHB. GABA system downregulation underlies the seizure risk of depressant withdrawal. Manages anxiety and neural excitability.
🔴
Glutamate
C₅H₉NO₄
The primary excitatory neurotransmitter. NMDA glutamate receptors are blocked by dissociatives (ketamine, PCP). Glutamate dysregulation underlies addiction-related neuroplasticity, memory consolidation, and psychotic disorders.
🟣
Norepinephrine
C₈H₁₁NO₃
The stress and arousal neurotransmitter. Elevated by stimulants, causing increased heart rate, blood pressure, and alertness. Critical in the sympathetic nervous system's "fight-or-flight" response and opioid withdrawal symptoms.
🟢
Endocannabinoids
AEA / 2-AG
The endogenous cannabinoids anandamide and 2-arachidonoylglycerol act as retrograde messengers, modulating synaptic transmission. THC mimics their effects at CB1/CB2 receptors, disrupting normal neuromodulatory signaling.
🩷
Endorphins
Opioid Peptides
Endogenous opioid peptides (β-endorphin, enkephalins, dynorphins) bind to opioid receptors, mediating analgesia, euphoria, and social bonding. Exogenous opioids mimic and overwhelm this system, leading to endorphin suppression.
🧡
Acetylcholine
C₇H₁₆NO₂⁺
Essential for learning, memory, and muscle control. Nicotine activates nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs) throughout the brain, stimulating dopamine release. Anticholinergic drugs (deliriants) block these receptors, causing severe delirium.
05 — Structural Chemistry

The Molecular Architecture of Psychoactivity

The pharmacological properties of drugs are intimately tied to their chemical structure. Small structural modifications can dramatically alter potency, selectivity, duration of action, and metabolic stability. This is the foundation of medicinal chemistry and explains how pharmaceutical companies develop novel drugs from natural templates.

LSD's ergoline core mimics the structural features of serotonin, enabling binding to multiple serotonin receptor subtypes. IUPAC: (RS)-1-(1,3-benzodioxol-5-yl)-N-methyl-propan-2-amine
MDMA's phenethylamine backbone places it in the amphetamine class, but its 3,4-methylenedioxy substituent gives it a unique pharmacological profile favoring serotonin over dopamine release. The mechanism involves reverse transport through SERT (serotonin transporter) — MDMA enters the axon terminal and triggers massive serotonin efflux. The R-enantiomer has greater entactogenic effects; the S-enantiomer has greater stimulant properties.

Δ9-THC
IUPAC: (6aR,10aR)-6,6,9-trimethyl-3-pentyl-6a,7,8,10a-tetrahydro-6H-benzo[c]chromen-1-ol
THC's terpenophenolic structure is highly lipophilic (LogP ~7), explaining its rapid distribution into fatty tissues including the brain and its extremely long elimination half-life. The pentyl side chain is essential for CB1 binding — longer chains (as in some synthetic cannabinoids) increase potency dramatically. Synthetic cannabinoids can be 100–1000× more potent than THC, producing dangerous toxicity in users expecting cannabis-like effects.

C₁₃H₁₆ClNO
Ketamine
IUPAC: (RS)-2-(2-chlorophenyl)-2-(methylamino)cyclohexan-1-one
Ketamine is a racemic mixture of R and S enantiomers. The S-enantiomer (esketamine/Spravato) is ~3–4× more potent at NMDA receptors and is the FDA-approved antidepressant form. The arylcyclohexylamine scaffold places ketamine in the same chemical class as PCP, but the 2-chlorophenyl group and methylamino substituent modulate its pharmacology to be less toxic than PCP. Its unique antidepressant mechanism may involve BDNF release and synaptogenesis independent of direct NMDA blockade.

06 — Health Consequences

The Human Cost

Drug misuse causes a spectrum of health consequences ranging from acute toxicity to chronic organ damage, mental illness, and death. The harms vary enormously by substance, dose, route of administration, frequency of use, individual vulnerability, and social context.

Cardiovascular System

  • Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamine): tachycardia, hypertension, coronary vasospasm
  • Cocaine-induced myocardial infarction in young adults with no prior disease
  • Inhalant use: sudden sniffing death syndrome via cardiac arrhythmia
  • MDMA: hypertensive crisis, QT prolongation
  • Opioids: bradycardia, hypotension

Respiratory System

  • Opioids: respiratory depression, hypoxia, apnea
  • Cannabis smoking: bronchitis, impaired mucociliary clearance
  • Crack cocaine: "crack lung" — acute pulmonary hemorrhage
  • Methamphetamine: pulmonary hypertension
  • Inhalants: pulmonary toxicity from solvents

Neurological System

  • Alcohol: blackouts, Wernicke encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency)
  • Stimulants: cerebrovascular accidents (strokes) in young users
  • LSD: persistent psychosis in susceptible individuals (rare)
  • MDMA: serotonin syndrome at high doses or with other serotonergic drugs
  • PCP: severe acute psychosis, extreme agitation

Thermoregulation & Electrolytes

  • MDMA: hyperthermia (temperatures >41°C / 106°F can be fatal)
  • Hyponatremia (dangerously low sodium) from excess water intake with MDMA
  • Stimulants: hyperthermia and rhabdomyolysis (muscle breakdown)
  • GHB overdose: hypothermia, coma
  • Alcohol: hypothermia in cold environments

Hepatic & Gastrointestinal

  • Alcohol: steatohepatitis → cirrhosis → hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Alcohol causes 50% of all cirrhosis cases globally
  • MDMA: acute hepatotoxicity (idiosyncratic)
  • IV drug use: viral hepatitis B and C — 67% of HCV cases in US linked to IDU
  • Opioids: chronic severe constipation, narcotic bowel syndrome

Immune System & Infectious Disease

  • Injection drug use: HIV, Hepatitis B/C, bacterial endocarditis
  • ~50% of new HIV infections linked to injection drug use
  • Methamphetamine: immune system suppression, skin sores
  • Cocaine insufflation: nasal septum perforation, mucosal damage
  • Alcohol: impaired immune surveillance, increased pneumonia risk

Neurological Damage

  • Alcohol: Korsakoff syndrome — severe, irreversible amnesia
  • Inhalants: white matter demyelination and cerebellar atrophy
  • Methamphetamine: striatal dopaminergic axon terminal loss
  • Chronic cannabis use in adolescents: reduced cortical thickness
  • Benzodiazepines: persistent cognitive deficits after cessation

Endocrine & Reproductive

  • Opioids: hypogonadism, infertility (via hypothalamic-pituitary axis suppression)
  • Anabolic steroids: testicular atrophy, gynecomastia, aggression
  • Cannabis: potential effects on testosterone and sperm motility
  • Prenatal drug exposure: fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASDs), neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome
  • Alcohol: fetal alcohol syndrome — leading preventable cause of intellectual disability

Psychosis & Schizophrenia

  • Cannabis: doubles the risk of psychosis in genetically vulnerable individuals (COMT Val158Met)
  • Methamphetamine psychosis: clinically indistinguishable from schizophrenia
  • Cocaine: paranoid psychosis with tactile hallucinations ("formication")
  • PCP/ketamine: model for understanding schizophrenia (glutamate hypothesis)
  • LSD: acute psychosis — typically resolves; chronic cases are rare but exist

Depression & Anxiety

  • Post-stimulant depression ("crash"): profound dysphoria as dopamine depletes
  • MDMA comedowns: days of serotonin depletion, anxiety, depression
  • Alcohol: induces depression with chronic use; commonly co-occurs with anxiety disorders
  • Cannabis: anxiety and panic reactions, particularly in high-THC products
  • Benzodiazepine withdrawal: severe rebound anxiety and panic disorder

HPPD & Persisting Effects

  • Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder (HPPD): visual snow, geometric patterns, after images — rare but documented
  • Post-acute withdrawal syndrome (PAWS): months to years of cognitive, emotional symptoms after cessation
  • Amotivational syndrome: debated cannabis-related syndrome of reduced motivation
  • Cocaine-induced deficits in working memory and attention persist years after cessation

Dual Diagnosis & Comorbidity

  • 50–80% of people with substance use disorders have co-occurring mental health conditions
  • Psychiatric disorders (depression, PTSD, ADHD) dramatically increase addiction risk
  • Self-medication hypothesis: individuals use substances to manage untreated mental illness
  • Integrated treatment (treating both disorders simultaneously) is most effective
  • Trauma history is one of the strongest predictors of substance use disorder
⚠️

Medical Emergency: Drug overdose is a life-threatening emergency. Opioid overdose can be reversed with naloxone (Narcan). Call emergency services immediately. Signs of opioid overdose: unresponsive, slow/stopped breathing, blue lips, pinpoint pupils.

Opioid Overdose — Most Deadly

  • Mechanism: μ-receptor activation in brainstem respiratory control centers
  • Cardinal triad: pinpoint pupils, unconsciousness, respiratory depression
  • Death from hypoxic brain injury within minutes
  • Fentanyl: extremely narrow therapeutic index; 2mg is potentially lethal
  • Reversal: naloxone (0.4–2mg IV/IM/IN) — may need multiple doses for fentanyl
  • Recovery position prevents aspiration death

Stimulant Toxicity

  • Hyperthermia: core temperature >41°C — primary cause of death
  • Cardiovascular: aortic dissection, myocardial infarction, arrhythmia
  • Cerebrovascular: hemorrhagic and ischemic stroke
  • Excited delirium syndrome: hyperthermia + agitation + death
  • No specific antidote — supportive care (cooling, benzodiazepines for seizures)

Serotonin Syndrome

  • Occurs when multiple serotonergic drugs are combined (MDMA + SSRI)
  • Triad: altered mental status, autonomic instability, neuromuscular abnormalities
  • Hunter criteria for diagnosis: specific clinical assessment tool
  • Severe cases: hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, multi-organ failure, death
  • Treatment: cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist), benzodiazepines, cooling

Depressant Overdose & Withdrawal

  • GHB/alcohol/benzodiazepine overdose: profound CNS and respiratory depression
  • Alcohol + benzodiazepine synergy: both enhance GABA-A — dangerous combination
  • Flumazenil reverses benzodiazepine overdose but can precipitate seizures
  • Alcohol withdrawal: delirium tremens (DTs) — mortality 5–15% untreated
  • Management: benzodiazepine-based CIWA protocol, thiamine, fluid support

08 — Society, Law & Policy

Drug Policy in the 21st Century

Drug policy represents one of the most contested areas of public health and criminal justice. The global debate has shifted from pure criminalization toward harm reduction, decriminalization, and even regulated legalization — driven by evidence that prohibition alone has failed.

The Case for Harm Reduction

Harm reduction is a public health approach that accepts drug use as a reality and seeks to minimize its negative consequences without requiring abstinence. Pioneered in the Netherlands in the 1980s, harm reduction encompasses needle exchange programs (reducing HIV transmission), supervised injection facilities, naloxone distribution, drug checking services, and safe messaging guidelines.

Evidence consistently demonstrates that harm reduction saves lives and reduces disease transmission without increasing drug use. Vancouver's Insite — the first supervised injection facility in North America — has facilitated over 3.6 million injections with zero on-site deaths, while linking clients to treatment services.

"We cannot arrest our way out of the drug problem. The alternative is harm reduction, treating addiction as a health issue."
— Kofi Annan, Former UN Secretary-General

Evidence-based harm reduction tools include: naloxone (opioid overdose reversal), fentanyl test strips, heroin-assisted treatment (HAT), and opioid agonist therapy (methadone/buprenorphine). Countries that have embraced comprehensive harm reduction — notably Switzerland, Portugal, and Germany — have seen dramatic reductions in overdose deaths and blood-borne disease transmission.

International Policy Approaches

🇵🇹
Portugal — Since 2001
Full Decriminalization
Possession of all drugs decriminalized for personal use. Drug-related offenses treated as administrative health matters, not crimes. HIV infections and overdose deaths fell dramatically. Drug use rates remain among Europe's lowest.
🇨🇭
Switzerland — Since 1994
Heroin-Assisted Treatment
Severe heroin addicts receive pharmaceutical-grade heroin in medical settings. Crime rates, HIV transmission, and homelessness among participants fell 60–80%. Now expanded to Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, Canada.
🇺🇸

24 states + DC have legalized recreational cannabis. Federal Schedule I status remains, creating legal contradictions. Tax revenues fund education and public health. Racial disparity in enforcement partially addressed.
🇩🇪

Became the largest European country to legalize cannabis possession (up to 25g) and home cultivation. Social clubs permit regulated distribution. Medical cannabis fully covered by insurance since 2017.

09 — Treatment & Recovery

Pathways to Recovery

Addiction is a complex, chronic brain disorder — not a moral failing. Like other chronic diseases (hypertension, diabetes), it requires long-term management combining pharmacological, behavioral, and social interventions. Relapse is part of the recovery process, not treatment failure.

01
💊
Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)
FDA-approved medications are first-line treatments for opioid and alcohol use disorders. For opioids: methadone (full μ-agonist, dispensed in OTPs), buprenorphine/naloxone (Suboxone — partial agonist, prescribable by certified physicians), and naltrexone (antagonist — Vivitrol injection). For alcohol: naltrexone, acamprosate, and disulfiram. MAT reduces cravings, withdrawal, and overdose mortality by up to 60%.
⬤ GOLD STANDARD FOR OUD & AUD
02
🧠
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
CBT is the most evidence-based psychotherapy for substance use disorders. It targets the cognitive distortions and behavioral patterns that maintain drug use. Core components include functional analysis of use, coping skills training, thought monitoring, and relapse prevention. CBT changes maladaptive neural patterns in the PFC and limbic system. It is effective for cocaine, cannabis, alcohol, and stimulant disorders where MAT options are limited.
⬤ 12–20 WEEKLY SESSIONS
03
🌿
Contingency Management (CM)
CM is a behavioral intervention with the highest level of evidence for stimulant use disorders (for which no effective medication exists). It uses systematic voucher-based incentives or prize draws contingent on verified drug abstinence. The approach harnesses natural dopamine reward mechanisms to reinforce sobriety. Meta-analyses show it doubles rates of abstinence compared to standard care. The PRIZE model has been successfully implemented in US Veterans Affairs clinics.
⬤ 12–24 WEEKS
04
✨
Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy
Emerging evidence supports psilocybin-assisted therapy for alcohol and tobacco use disorders, with cessation rates far exceeding standard treatments. MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD — which frequently underlies substance use — showed 67–71% PTSD remission in Phase 3 trials. The mechanism involves increased neuroplasticity (BDNF upregulation), enhanced therapeutic alliance, and access to repressed emotional material. These treatments are administered in carefully structured therapeutic sessions, not as standalone drug interventions.
⬤ EMERGING — 1–3 SESSIONS + INTEGRATION
05
👥
Mutual Aid & 12-Step Programs
Alcoholics Anonymous (est. 1935) and Narcotics Anonymous remain the most widely accessed recovery support globally. Research demonstrates that AA attendance is associated with better outcomes, likely through social support, accountability, and structured behavioral change. SMART Recovery offers a science-based, non-spiritual alternative. Peer support specialists — people with lived experience of addiction — are increasingly recognized as essential to recovery ecosystems.
⬤ ONGOING — LIFETIME SUPPORT
06
🏥
Residential & Medically Supervised Detox
Medically supervised detoxification is essential for opioid, alcohol, and benzodiazepine withdrawal. Abrupt cessation of CNS depressants can be life-threatening — medical detox protocols use tapering schedules (benzodiazepines for alcohol/benzo withdrawal; buprenorphine for opioids). Residential treatment programs provide 30–90 days of intensive, structured care addressing medical, psychological, and social needs. Evidence is mixed but residential care suits those with severe disorders or unstable home environments.
⬤ 7–90 DAYS INTENSIVE

Critical Questions in Addiction Medicine

Is addiction a disease or a choice? +
The scientific consensus, endorsed by NIDA, ASAM, APA, and WHO, classifies addiction as a chronic brain disorder with genetic, developmental, and environmental determinants. Neuroimaging consistently shows structural and functional brain differences in people with addiction. However, the disease model does not eliminate agency — recovery requires sustained effort, behavioral change, and social support. The debate is partly semantic: even if use begins with a choice, compulsive use emerges from neurobiological changes that powerfully constrain subsequent choices. A nuanced view recognizes both biological vulnerability and the capacity for change. Critically, the disease framing reduces stigma and increases treatment-seeking, while the moral model contributes to undertreatment and discrimination.
Why do only some users become addicted? +
Addiction susceptibility is determined by a complex interplay of genetic (~40–60% heritability for most substances), developmental (adolescent brain is particularly vulnerable — drugs used before age 18 carry 4–7× the addiction risk of adult onset), psychological (trauma, mental illness, adverse childhood experiences), and social (poverty, peer use, drug availability) factors. Specific genetic variants affect drug metabolism (CYP2D6 polymorphisms), receptor sensitivity (OPRM1 variants for opioids, COMT variants affecting dopamine metabolism), and stress response systems. The vast majority of people who try most drugs do not develop addiction — approximately 9% of cannabis users, 23% of heroin users, 17% of cocaine users, and 32% of nicotine users develop dependence.
Can cannabis cause psychosis? +
The evidence is clear and dose-dependent: cannabis use is associated with increased risk of psychosis, particularly in genetically vulnerable individuals. A landmark meta-analysis found that heavy cannabis use approximately doubles the risk of schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. The risk is greatest with high-potency cannabis (THC ≥15%), early onset of use (before age 15), frequency of use, and in carriers of the COMT Val158Met polymorphism (which reduces dopamine metabolism in the PFC). The causal mechanism likely involves THC-induced perturbation of dopaminergic and glutamatergic neurotransmission in developing corticolimbic circuits. Critically, CBD appears to have antipsychotic properties that may counteract THC — the removal of CBD from high-potency cannabis products is a public health concern. Approximately 30% of psychosis cases in urban Europe may be attributable to high-potency cannabis use.
What is the difference between physical and psychological dependence? +
Physical dependence refers to neuroadaptations that cause a characteristic withdrawal syndrome upon cessation — the body has physically adapted to the drug's presence. It occurs with opioids, alcohol, benzodiazepines, nicotine, and some other substances. Crucially, physical dependence can occur without addiction — patients taking prescribed opioids for chronic pain may be physically dependent but do not have addiction (compulsive use despite harm). Psychological dependence refers to the cognitive and emotional dimensions of craving, preoccupation with obtaining the drug, and the subjective sense of "needing" the substance for well-being. Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamine) produce powerful psychological dependence with minimal physical withdrawal. Modern addiction science has moved beyond this binary, recognizing that all drug dependence involves neurobiological changes that blur the distinction between "physical" and "psychological."
Are psychedelics the future of psychiatry? +
The evidence is cautiously optimistic. Phase 2 and 3 clinical trials have demonstrated remarkable efficacy for psilocybin-assisted therapy in treatment-resistant depression (60-80% response rates vs 20-30% for standard SSRIs), MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD (67-71% no longer meeting diagnostic criteria at 18-month follow-up), and psilocybin for addiction (80% smoking cessation at 12 months vs 35% for standard care). These results have led to FDA Breakthrough Therapy designations. The mechanisms involve: (1) increased neuroplasticity via BDNF and dendritic spine density increases; (2) disruption of maladaptive default mode network (DMN) activity associated with depression and rumination; (3) the therapeutic alliance and psychological work enabled by the altered state. Challenges include integration into existing healthcare systems, training therapists, ensuring equitable access, and managing the rare but real risks of adverse psychological reactions. Australia approved the clinical use of psilocybin and MDMA in 2024 — other countries are watching closely.
10 — Scientific Glossary

Key Terms in Pharmacology & Addiction Science

Agonist
A drug that activates a receptor and produces a biological response. Full agonists produce maximal effect; partial agonists produce submaximal effect even at full receptor occupancy (e.g., buprenorphine at μ-opioid receptors).
Antagonist
A drug that binds to a receptor but produces no response, while blocking agonist binding. Naloxone is a competitive opioid receptor antagonist, reversing opioid overdose.
Allosteric Modulator
A drug that binds to a receptor at a site other than the primary agonist binding site, changing the receptor's conformation and response to the endogenous ligand. Benzodiazepines are positive allosteric modulators of GABA-A.
Half-Life (t½)
The time required for the plasma concentration of a drug to reduce by 50%. Approximately 5 half-lives are required for near-complete elimination. Determines dosing frequency and duration of effect.
Bioavailability
The fraction of administered drug that reaches systemic circulation unchanged. IV administration = 100% bioavailability. Oral, inhaled, intranasal routes have variable bioavailability due to first-pass metabolism, absorption limitations.
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections. Drugs alter neuroplasticity both acutely (LSD acutely increases dendritic spine density) and chronically (addiction-related structural changes in PFC, NAc, amygdala).
Tolerance
A reduced pharmacological response to a drug following repeated exposure. Mechanisms include: receptor downregulation, desensitization, and adaptive neurobiological changes. Requires increasing doses to achieve the same effect.
Cross-Tolerance
Tolerance to one drug that confers tolerance to another drug with similar mechanism. Classical psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, mescaline) show mutual cross-tolerance via 5-HT2A receptor. Opioids show cross-tolerance with other opioids.
Therapeutic Index (TI)
Ratio of toxic dose to therapeutic dose (TD50/ED50). A narrow TI indicates little margin between therapeutic and toxic doses. Opioids have narrow TI; benzodiazepines have wide TI (safer in overdose alone, dangerous combined with other depressants).
Prodrug
An inactive compound that is metabolized in vivo to an active form. Psilocybin is a prodrug — dephosphorylated by alkaline phosphatase to active psilocin. Codeine is a prodrug converted by CYP2D6 to morphine.
Default Mode Network
A set of brain regions (mPFC, PCC, angular gyrus) active during self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and rumination. Overactive in depression and addiction. Psychedelics produce dramatic DMN disruption, possibly explaining their therapeutic effects.
Serotonin Syndrome
A potentially life-threatening drug interaction caused by excess serotonergic activity. Triad: altered mentation, autonomic dysfunction (hyperthermia, tachycardia), neuromuscular abnormalities (clonus, hyperreflexia). Risk with MDMA + MAOIs, SSRIs, triptans.
ADME
Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion — the four pharmacokinetic processes determining drug concentration over time. CYP450 enzymes (primarily CYP3A4, CYP2D6) are responsible for metabolizing the majority of psychoactive drugs.
Blood-Brain Barrier
A highly selective semipermeable border of endothelial cells protecting the brain from pathogens and toxins. Drugs must have appropriate lipophilicity, size, and charge to cross. High lipophilicity generally aids BBB penetration (THC, heroin).
DSM-5 SUD
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (5th ed.) defines Substance Use Disorder as a maladaptive pattern of use causing clinically significant impairment/distress, assessed across 11 criteria (craving, tolerance, withdrawal, loss of control, etc.) — rated mild (2-3), moderate (4-5), or severe (6+).
ΔFosB
A truncated splice variant of the FosB transcription factor. Accumulates with repeated drug exposure in the NAc and persists for weeks/months. Acts as a "molecular switch" for addiction-related gene expression changes. Biomarker of addiction chronicity.
Polypharmacy / Poly-drug Use
The concurrent use of multiple psychoactive substances. Dramatically increases adverse event risk through pharmacodynamic synergism (e.g., opioid + benzodiazepine = additive respiratory depression) and pharmacokinetic interactions (CYP450 inhibition/induction).
Naloxone (Narcan)
A competitive, high-affinity μ-opioid receptor antagonist with no intrinsic activity. Rapidly (within minutes) reverses opioid overdose. Intranasal formulation (Narcan nasal spray) is FDA-approved OTC. Half-life ~30–80 min — shorter than many opioids, requiring re-dosing.
